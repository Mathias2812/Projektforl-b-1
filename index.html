Disparities in Plastic Waste Management

The bar chart reveals that both the Solomon Islands and Suriname mismanage 100% of their plastic waste, with Mozambique, Madagascar, and Uganda following closely behind. This indicates that all plastic waste produced in these countries ends up as litter, in landfills, or in the ocean and will most likely causes harm to our environment.


Additionally, the world map highlights that African and other developing nations generally have high MWI scores, significantly contributing to the global plastic problem. However, the rest of the world generally scores low, hinting that they might have better resources to manage their plastic waste.


Consequently when some countries score at or close to 100%, it appears there is still some way to go to achieve the UN's 2030 goal of reducing environmental impact by focusing on improved waste management.

Only 9% of the world's plastic waste is being recycled

Although the graph reveals that plastic recycling has increased since 2000, its notable that only about 9% of the world's total plastic waste is being recycled.

Fate of plastic waste worldwide

Why aren't we recycling more plastic?

The chart reveals that roughly 50% of the worlds plastic waste is sent straight to landfill and another 22% littered, putting it at risk of being leaked into rivers, lakes, and the ocean. Other findings reveal that the majority of plastic waste in the United States is sent to landfills, whereas incineration rates are high in Europe.


Looking at the chart, it is evident that plastic waste management strategies vary significantly around the globe. Inefficient waste management strategies might be part of the problem, but the world's plastic crisis is complex and requires innovative solutions. Contamination and harmful chemicals complicate recycling, and the quality of plastic degrades after each recycling process. Most plastics can only be recycled once or twice.
